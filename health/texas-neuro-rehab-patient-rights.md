### 2.2 Your Core Patient Rights

Under **26 Tex. Admin. Code § 320.7** and **Texas Health & Safety Code § 576.021**, you have the following rights that cannot be waived by anyone — not the facility, not your attorney, not a guardian — unless a court has specifically ordered otherwise: [^3] [^4]

**Right to a Humane Treatment Environment**

> "The right to a humane treatment environment that ensures protection from harm, provides privacy to as great a degree as possible with regard to personal needs, and promotes respect and dignity for each individual." — 26 Tex. Admin. Code § 320.7(3)

This right directly applies to the denial of pillows and blankets. A humane treatment environment includes basic physical comfort. Withholding standard bedding items without a documented clinical justification is a potential violation of this right.

**Right to Refuse Medication**

Under **Texas Health & Safety Code § 462.009**, you have the right to refuse any medication, therapy, or treatment. The facility may only override this refusal in three narrow circumstances: [^5]

1. You are a minor and your parent or guardian consents on your behalf.
2. A court has adjudicated you as incompetent to manage your personal affairs.
3. A physician determines in writing that the medication is **immediately necessary to prevent imminent serious physical harm** to you or another person.

If none of these conditions apply, you have the legal right to refuse any drug being administered to you. Your refusal must be documented in your clinical record, along with your stated reason.

**Right to Medical Testing and Independent Evaluation**

Under **26 Tex. Admin. Code § 320.7(11)**, you have the right to obtain an independent medical examination or evaluation by a physician of your choice, at your own expense, at any reasonable time. The facility must allow this. [^3]

Additionally, under **§ 320.7(8)**, you have the right to receive explanations of the care and procedures to be provided, and the right to know about alternative treatment procedures available to you. If an MRI is a medically appropriate diagnostic tool for your back condition, the facility is obligated to either provide it, explain why they are not providing it, and inform you of alternatives.

**Right to Participate in Your Treatment Plan**

You have the right to actively participate in the development and periodic review of your individualized treatment plan. You cannot be excluded from this process. [^3]

**Right to Be Free from Unnecessary Medication**

> "The right to be free from unnecessary or excessive medication, which includes the right to give or withhold informed consent to treatment with psychoactive medication, unless the right has been limited by court order or in an emergency." — 26 Tex. Admin. Code § 320.7(16)

**Right to File a Complaint Without Retaliation**

> "The right of any individual to make a complaint regarding denial of rights without any form of retaliation." — 26 Tex. Admin. Code § 320.7(31)

The facility cannot punish you, restrict your privileges, or retaliate against you in any way for filing a complaint — internally or with an outside agency.

**Right to Know Your Rights**

These rights must be explained to you aloud within 24 hours of admission. If this did not happen, that itself is a violation. [^3]

## Part 3: How to Demand Discharge

### 3.1 Voluntary vs. Involuntary Status

The most important thing to determine is whether you were admitted **voluntarily** or are being held **involuntarily**. This fundamentally changes your rights.

If you signed admission paperwork voluntarily, you are a **voluntary patient** and have the right to request discharge at any time. The facility cannot simply refuse — they must follow a specific legal process if they want to keep you.

### 3.2 The Voluntary Discharge Process (Step-by-Step)

Under **Texas Health & Safety Code § 572.004** and **26 Tex. Admin. Code § 568.83**, the process is as follows: [^6] [^7]

**Step 1 — Submit a Written Request**

Ask a staff member for a **Discharge Request Form**. If they do not provide one, write a statement yourself on any paper that says: *"I, [your name], hereby request discharge from Texas NeuroRehab Center. Date: [date]. Signature: [your signature]."* Give it to the facility administrator or the charge nurse and keep a copy if possible.

If you make a verbal request to leave, a staff member is legally required to help you prepare a written request as soon as possible.

**Step 2 — The Four-Hour Window**

Within **four hours** of receiving your written request, the facility must notify your treating physician. The physician must then authorize your discharge before the end of that four-hour period — unless they have "reasonable cause" to believe you meet the criteria for involuntary commitment.

**Step 3 — If the Physician Objects**

If the physician believes you should not be discharged, they must:
- Notify you of their intent to detain you beyond four hours.
- Re-examine you within **24 hours** to determine what further services you need.
- If they still do not discharge you, they must file an application for **court-ordered mental health services** by 4 p.m. on the next business day after that re-examination.

**Step 4 — Court Involvement**

Even if the physician files for court-ordered commitment, a **judge** — not the facility — makes the final decision on whether you can be held. You have the right to legal representation in that hearing.

### 3.3 Important Practical Steps

- **Document everything.** Write down dates, times, names of staff, and exactly what was said to you.
- **Contact Disability Rights Texas immediately** if your discharge request is denied or ignored: **1-800-252-9108**. They can provide legal advocacy in real time.
- **Ask to speak with the facility's Rights Protection Officer.** Every licensed psychiatric facility in Texas is required to have one. This person's job is to protect your legal rights within the facility.
- **Contact a family member or trusted person** and ask them to advocate on your behalf and, if necessary, to contact the agencies listed below.

## Part 4: How and Where to File Complaints

Texas NeuroRehab is subject to oversight by multiple agencies. Filing with all of them simultaneously creates the strongest paper trail and increases the likelihood of a formal investigation.

### 4.1 Texas Health and Human Services Commission (HHSC) — Health Facility Compliance

This is the **primary state agency** that licenses and regulates healthcare facilities in Texas. HHSC investigates complaints about substandard care, patient rights violations, and unsafe conditions. [^8]

| Contact Method | Details |
|---|---|
| **Online Portal (TULIP)** | Available 24/7 at hhs.texas.gov — search "TULIP Complaints" |
| **Phone** | **1-800-458-9858** |
| **Email** | hfc.complaints@hhs.texas.gov |
| **Fax** | 833-709-5735 |
| **Mail** | Health and Human Services Commission, Complaint and Incident Intake, Mail Code E-249, P.O. Box 149030, Austin, TX 78714-9030 |

**What to include in your complaint:** Your name and contact information (or file anonymously), the facility's name and address, specific dates and descriptions of each incident, the names of any staff involved, and the outcome you are seeking.

**What happens next:** HHSC will evaluate whether a regulatory violation exists and may conduct an on-site or mail investigation. You will be notified of the findings.

### 4.2 The Joint Commission — Office of Quality and Patient Safety

Texas NeuroRehab holds Joint Commission accreditation. Filing a complaint here puts the facility's accreditation status at risk, which is a significant lever. [^9]

| Contact Method | Details |
|---|---|
| **Online Form** | jointcommission.org → "Report a Patient Safety Event" |
| **Phone** | **1-800-994-6610** |
| **Mail** | Office of Quality and Patient Safety, The Joint Commission, One Renaissance Boulevard, Oakbrook Terrace, IL 60181 |

**Note:** The Joint Commission does not accept faxed or emailed submissions. They also cannot share the results of their review with you, but the complaint goes on record and can trigger an unannounced survey of the facility.

### 4.3 Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS)

Because Texas NeuroRehab is a Medicare-certified facility, CMS has jurisdiction over its compliance with federal Conditions of Participation, which include extensive patient rights protections. [^10]

| Contact Method | Details |
|---|---|
| **Online** | medicare.gov → "Filing a Complaint" |
| **Phone** | **1-800-MEDICARE (1-800-633-4227)** |
| **State Survey Agency** | CMS routes hospital complaints through the Texas HHSC (same as above) |

For improper care or unsafe conditions in a medical facility, CMS directs complainants to contact the **State Survey Agency**, which in Texas is HHSC. Filing with both CMS and HHSC ensures the complaint is logged at both the federal and state level.

### 4.4 Texas Department of Family and Protective Services (TDFPS) — Abuse and Neglect

If you believe you are experiencing **abuse or neglect** — which can include withholding basic necessities like bedding — you can report this to TDFPS. [^11]

| Contact Method | Details |

### 4.5 Disability Rights Texas

Disability Rights Texas (DRTx) is the federally designated **protection and advocacy agency** for people with disabilities in Texas. They can provide free legal assistance, file complaints on your behalf, and advocate for your immediate release if your rights are being violated. [^11]
